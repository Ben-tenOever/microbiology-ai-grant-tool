Joel Belasco PhD

Bacteriology
Post transcriptional gene control
RNA biology

Specialty areas RNA stability and decay in bacteria, post transcriptional regulation, mRNA structure function, small RNA control of gene expression

Lab synopsis The Belasco lab dissects how RNA structure and RNA binding factors control the stability and translation of bacterial mRNAs. Using biochemical, genetic, and structural approaches they define how ribonucleases, small RNAs, and RNA protein complexes tune bacterial gene expression to respond to stress, nutrient availability, and host environments. These principles form the conceptual foundation for manipulating microbial gene expression in infection, microbiome engineering, and synthetic biology.

Research keywords mRNA turnover, RNA structure, endoribonucleases, small RNAs, stress responses, translational control, synthetic gene circuits in bacteria

Experimental capabilities Bacterial genetics and strain engineering, RNA biochemistry and footprinting, quantitative measurements of mRNA half life and translation, transcriptomics and ribosome profiling, in vitro reconstitution of RNA protein complexes

Complementary areas Synthetic biology and microbial circuit design, antimicrobial target discovery, host pathogen interactions where bacterial stress responses determine outcome

Model systems and disease focus Gram negative and Gram positive bacterial models of stress adaptation, with applications to infection biology and microbiome engineering

Example grant directions for this PI
  • NIAID and NIGMS programs focused on bacterial gene regulation and RNA biology
  • Synthetic biology initiatives that require rational control of microbial gene expression
  • Collaborations on antibiotic tolerance and persistence that hinge on stress induced RNA programs

Andrew J Darwin PhD

Bacteriology
Envelope stress and secretion

Specialty areas Bacterial envelope stress responses, protein translocation systems, secretion associated virulence

Lab synopsis The Andrew Darwin lab studies how Gram negative bacteria sense and respond to damage at the cell envelope. They focus especially on the phage shock protein system and related pathways that monitor outer membrane integrity during infection, antibiotic exposure, and host immune attack. By defining the molecular logic of these stress responses they reveal vulnerabilities that can be targeted to sensitize pathogens to host defense or antimicrobial therapy.

Research keywords Envelope stress signaling, phage shock protein system, secretion machinery, outer membrane biogenesis, virulence regulation

Experimental capabilities Classical and modern bacterial genetics, protein localization and topology mapping, membrane protein biochemistry, infection relevant stress models, fluorescence based reporters of envelope damage

Complementary areas Antimicrobial resistance and tolerance programs, innate immune sensing of envelope damage, combinations with host directed therapies that disrupt bacterial envelopes

Model systems and disease focus Gram negative pathogens including Yersinia and Pseudomonas models of systemic and respiratory infection

Example grant directions for this PI
  • NIAID mechanisms focused on bacterial virulence and stress responses
  • Cross department efforts on antimicrobial resistance and host directed therapeutics

K Heran Darwin PhD

Bacteriology
Mycobacteria and proteasomes

Specialty areas Mycobacterium tuberculosis pathogenesis, bacterial proteasomes, persistence and host immune evasion

Lab synopsis The Heran Darwin lab investigates how the mycobacterial proteasome and associated protein quality control pathways shape tuberculosis virulence and persistence. They use genetics and chemical biology to define how selective protein degradation allows M tuberculosis to withstand host derived stresses, including reactive nitrogen species and nutrient limitation, and to establish chronic infection.

Research keywords Mycobacterial proteasome, protein degradation, stress adaptation, chronic infection, tuberculosis immunology

Experimental capabilities BSL3 mycobacterial culture and genetics, infection models of TB, proteomics of ubiquitin like modifications, small molecule targeting of bacterial proteasomes, host pathogen interaction assays

Complementary areas Global health focused TB therapeutics, innate immune sensing of bacterial metabolic stress, host directed therapies that interface with mycobacterial quality control pathways

Model systems and disease focus Mycobacterium tuberculosis infection models ranging from in vitro macrophages to murine and other in vivo systems of pulmonary TB

Example grant directions for this PI
  • NIAID programs in tuberculosis pathogenesis and therapeutics
  • Global health foundations focused on TB drug discovery and resistance

Mark Garabedian PhD

Oncogenesis
Nuclear receptor signaling

Specialty areas Nuclear receptor biology, androgen receptor and other steroid receptors, transcriptional control in cancer especially prostate

Lab synopsis The Garabedian lab explores how nuclear receptors interpret hormonal and environmental signals to reprogram gene expression in cancer. They focus particularly on androgen receptor signaling in prostate cancer and how crosstalk between nuclear receptors and chromatin regulators drives tumor progression, therapy resistance, and lineage plasticity. Their work links fundamental receptor biology to biomarker and drug development.

Research keywords Nuclear receptors, androgen receptor, chromatin remodeling, transcription complexes, hormone driven cancer, therapy resistance

Experimental capabilities Cancer cell line and organoid models, CRISPR based genomic editing, ChIP and chromatin profiling, transcriptional and epigenomic analysis, high content imaging, functional screens for nuclear receptor modulators

Complementary areas Prostate and other hormone driven malignancies, inflammation and nuclear receptor crosstalk, systems level analysis of tumor microenvironments where hormones intersect with immunity

Model systems and disease focus Prostate cancer models including androgen sensitive and castration resistant states with opportunities for cross talk with immunology and infection focused projects

Example grant directions for this PI
  • NCI programs in nuclear receptor signaling and hormone driven cancers
  • Collaborative projects on immunotherapy resistance that involves nuclear receptor pathways

Kamal Khanna PhD

Immunology
Tissue resident T cells
Mucosal immunity

Specialty areas Tissue resident and mucosal T cell biology, immune memory in barrier tissues, infection and inflammation in the lung and gut

Lab synopsis The Khanna lab defines how tissue resident T cells and other lymphocyte populations are established, maintained, and recalled in barrier sites such as the lung and intestine. They integrate infection models, fate mapping, and single cell profiling to reveal how local cues imprint long lived immune memory and how these programs are altered in chronic inflammation and aging.

Research keywords Tissue resident memory T cells, mucosal immunity, lung and gut infection, immune memory imprinting, aging, barrier immunology

Experimental capabilities Murine models of respiratory and enteric infection, advanced flow cytometry and spectral cytometry, fate mapping and reporter mice, tissue imaging, single cell transcriptomics of immune populations

Complementary areas Viral and bacterial infection models in the lung and intestine, vaccine design that targets tissue resident memory, inflammatory and fibrotic lung disease including post viral sequelae

Model systems and disease focus Mouse models of acute and chronic infection at mucosal surfaces with links to human tissue studies through collaborations

Example grant directions for this PI
  • NIAID programs on tissue resident immunity and host protection at barrier sites
  • Multi project grants on lung inflammation, including RESPOND style efforts
